The Explosive Regime: α < −2

Noam Borgnia · Thesis work under Prof. Noam Berger, TU Munich

For strongly negative α, large clusters grow instantaneously relative to small ones. Once a cluster acquires a slight size advantage, it accelerates relative to its neighbors, sweeping up nearby particles and growing even faster. In infinite volume this leads to explosion — an infinite component forming in finite time — so the model is not well-defined on ℤ² for α < −2. Nevertheless, the geometry of the dominant cluster on a finite lattice is interesting and varies strikingly with the initial density p.

Since the dominant cluster moves so fast relative to everything else, the process effectively reduces to one cluster absorbing stationary particles. The geometry then depends on the density of those particles, and at each density the resulting cluster resembles a classical single-cluster growth model. In the simulations below, we seed a small 3×3 cluster at the origin to ensure the dominant cluster grows from the center, making the geometric comparison with the classical models more striking.

Low density → DLA

When particles are sparse, the dominant cluster must diffuse between absorptions, and approaching particles are more likely to contact the outermost tips of the cluster, producing ramified growth reminiscent of diffusion-limited aggregation (DLA). The correspondence becomes exact in the limit p → 0: each absorption adds a single particle after a long diffusion, the direction of approach is fully randomized, and the cluster's rigid-body random walk is equivalent to a particle arriving from infinity. At moderate p = 0.15 the geometry is qualitatively similar but not identical to DLA, since the cluster doesn't travel far enough between absorptions for each encounter to look truly independent.

Critical density → Percolation exploration

At p near the site percolation threshold (pc ≈ 0.59 on ℤ²), the dominant cluster effectively explores a random subgraph of occupied sites. It can only absorb a neighbor if that site is occupied, so the resulting geometry is governed by the connectivity structure of the underlying Bernoulli site percolation configuration. As with the Eden comparison, we use edge-weighted exploration: boundary sites with more cluster neighbors are proportionally more likely to be absorbed, matching the CCA translation mechanic.

Full density → Eden model

At p = 1, every neighbor of the dominant cluster is occupied, so the cluster grows by absorbing boundary sites. In the CCA dynamics, a boundary site adjacent to the cluster from multiple directions has proportionally more chances of being hit by a translation step — so the natural comparison is the edge-weighted Eden model (sometimes called bond FPP), where each boundary site is selected with probability proportional to its number of cluster neighbors. This produces compact, roughly circular clusters with a smooth boundary (up to KPZ-class fluctuations).
