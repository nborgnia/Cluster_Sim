The Explosive Regime: α < 0

Noam Borgnia · Thesis work under Prof. Noam Berger, TU Munich

For strongly negative α, large clusters move faster than small ones. Once a cluster acquires a slight size advantage, it accelerates relative to its neighbors, sweeping up nearby particles and growing even faster. In infinite volume this leads to explosion — an infinite component forming in finite time — so the model is not well-defined on ℤ² for α < 0. Nevertheless, the geometry of the dominant cluster on a finite lattice is interesting and varies strikingly with the initial density p.

At low density (p = 0.15), the dominant cluster must wander far between absorptions, producing a ramified, fractal structure with long tendrils. At moderate density (p = 0.5), the cluster explores a denser field and the geometry is intermediate. At full occupancy (p = 1), the cluster fills its neighborhood uniformly, producing a compact, roughly circular shape.

Comparison with classical growth models

These three regimes recall classical single-cluster growth models, and the correspondence is not a coincidence. When α is sufficiently negative, the mass dynamics ensure that only the dominant cluster moves on any given timescale — all other clusters are effectively frozen. The process then reduces to a single cluster absorbing stationary particles, and the geometry depends on the density of those particles:

Low density → DLA

When particles are sparse, the dominant cluster must diffuse a long distance between absorptions. Approaching particles are more likely to contact the outermost tips of the cluster (since those are the first sites a random walker encounters), producing highly ramified growth. This is the same mechanism underlying diffusion-limited aggregation (DLA), where particles arrive from infinity and stick on contact. The CCA cluster at low p should share the fractal dimension of 2D DLA (df ≈ 1.71).

Moderate density → Percolation exploration

At moderate p near the site percolation threshold (pc ≈ 0.59 on ℤ²), the dominant cluster effectively explores a random subgraph of occupied sites. It can only absorb a neighbor if that site is occupied, so the resulting geometry is governed by the connectivity structure of the underlying Bernoulli site percolation configuration. The cluster traces out something resembling the explored region of a percolation cluster.

High density → Eden model

At p = 1, every neighbor of the dominant cluster is occupied, so the cluster grows by absorbing a uniformly random boundary site at each step. This is precisely the Eden model of uniform random boundary growth, which produces compact, roughly circular clusters with a smooth boundary (up to KPZ-class fluctuations).
