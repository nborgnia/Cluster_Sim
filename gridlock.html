Gridlock and the Separation of Scales

Noam Borgnia · Thesis work under Prof. Noam Berger, TU Munich

For large $\alpha$, the CCA dynamics exhibit a striking hierarchical structure: clusters grow until they reach a mass at which they are effectively frozen on the current timescale, a tessellation of frozen clusters forms, and growth resumes only when the next (much slower) timescale becomes relevant. We call this phenomenon gridlock. It is the key mechanism behind the conjectured $t^{1/\alpha}$ growth rate.

The argument below is developed for full initial occupancy ($p = 1$), but the simulations suggest the same mechanism operates for all $p > 0$. The epoch viewer below illustrates gridlock at different time scales.

The frozen model at scale $M$

To analyze the gridlock mechanism, we introduce a simplified model. Fix a mass cutoff $M$. The frozen model at scale $M$ modifies the CCA dynamics so that any cluster reaching mass $\geq M$ is frozen: it no longer initiates jumps, but can still passively receive mobile clusters that merge into it.

For $M = 2$ with full initial occupancy on $\mathbb{Z}^2$, the dynamics are particularly clean. Each singleton has an independent $\operatorname{Exp}(1)$ clock. When it rings, the singleton picks one of its $4$ lattice neighbors uniformly at random and joins that neighbor's cluster. Once a cluster reaches mass $\geq 2$, it freezes.

Proposition (Branching domination at $M = 2$)

In the frozen model at scale $M = 2$ with full initial occupancy on $\mathbb{Z}^2$, the origin cluster $C_0$ satisfies $$|C_0| \preceq |\mathcal{T}_0|,$$ where $\mathcal{T}_0$ is a Galton–Watson tree with offspring distribution $\operatorname{Bin}(3, 1/4)$, and $\preceq$ denotes stochastic domination.

Proof.

We explore the boundary of $C_0$ in breadth-first order and couple the growth with a branching process. The root is the origin. At each step, we process the next boundary singleton $x$ adjacent to $C_0$ by flipping an independent $\operatorname{Ber}(1/4)$ coin:

On success (probability $1/4$): $x$ joins $C_0$, and its at most $3$ non-$C_0$ neighbors are added to the exploration queue as children of $x$. On failure (probability $3/4$): $x$ does not join $C_0$ and is a leaf.

The coupling overestimates growth for two reasons. First, each coin flip is an overestimate: in the true process, $x$ picks one of its $4$ neighbors uniformly, so the probability it picks the direction toward $C_0$ is at most $1/4$. Moreover, $x$ might be absorbed by a different cluster before its clock rings. Second, if $C_0$ grows around a site so that it is adjacent from two directions, the branching process gives it two independent chances to join (as a child in two branches), while the true process gives it only one.

Since each successful vertex contributes at most $3$ children, each succeeding independently with probability $\leq 1/4$, the offspring distribution is dominated by $\operatorname{Bin}(3, 1/4)$.

$\square$

The offspring mean is $\mu = 3/4 < 1$, so the tree is subcritical. Standard results (the Dwass–Kemperman formula) give $$\mathbb{P}(|C_0| \geq k) \leq C \cdot (3/4)^k \, k^{-3/2}$$ and $\mathbb{E}[|C_0|] = 1/(1 - 3/4) = 4$. So in the frozen model at $M = 2$, the origin cluster is almost surely finite with exponentially decaying tails.

Timescale separation and the multi-scale picture

The power of the frozen model comes from iterating it across scales. Consider the full (unfrozen) dynamics. We define dyadic scales: scale $n$ consists of clusters with mass in $[2^{n-1}, 2^n)$.

At scale $n$, the relevant clusters move at rate $\sim (2^n)^{-\alpha} = 2^{-n\alpha}$. The time needed for scale-$n$ dynamics to play out is $\sim 2^{n\alpha}$ (the inverse of the rate). At scale $n+1$, the rate is $2^{-(n+1)\alpha}$, and the timescale is $2^{(n+1)\alpha}$. The ratio between successive timescales is the constant $2^{\alpha}$: scale-$(n+1)$ dynamics are slower than scale-$n$ dynamics by a factor of $2^{\alpha}$. For large $\alpha$ this factor is large, providing clean separation; for moderate $\alpha$ the scales begin to overlap.

This separation of scales means that, to a good approximation, each scale completes its dynamics before the next one begins. At scale $1$, singletons merge into clusters of mass $\sim 2$. These then freeze (on the scale-$1$ timescale) and tile the plane. At scale $2$, the mass-$2$ clusters begin to merge — but now they behave like "super-singletons" on a coarsened lattice. The process repeats.

Remark (imperfect separation)

The timescale separation is not exact. During the scale-$n$ window (duration $\sim 2^{n\alpha}$), each scale-$(n+1)$ cluster fires at rate $2^{-(n+1)\alpha}$, so the probability of a premature scale-$(n+1)$ merger is $\sim 2^{n\alpha} \cdot 2^{-(n+1)\alpha} = 2^{-\alpha}$ — a constant independent of $n$. Each such premature merger enlarges the origin cluster and exposes new boundary at scale $n$, spawning fresh branches of the scale-$n$ branching process. Crucially, this $2^{-\alpha}$ leakage does not decay with scale, so the natural formalization as a multitype Galton–Watson process (with type = scale, and $2^{-\alpha}$ probability of producing a child one scale up) is not obviously subcritical — in fact, the constant immigration rate across scales causes the joint process to survive. This is a genuine obstruction, not merely a technicality, and it means the clean hierarchical picture requires additional input (such as the $O(1)$-neighbor property below) to be made rigorous even for large $\alpha$.

The geometric bottleneck

To push the frozen-model argument through multiple scales, we need a key geometric input: that the tessellation at each scale has bounded local complexity. Specifically, after the scale-$n$ dynamics complete, we need the origin cluster on the coarsened lattice to have $O(1)$ neighbors.

At scale $n$, each "child" in the branching process corresponds to a neighboring cluster of mass $\sim 2^n$ merging into the origin cluster. So each child contributes mass $\sim 2^n$, and the total mass gained at scale $n$ is $T_n \cdot 2^n$, where $T_n$ is the total progeny of the scale-$n$ branching process.

If the origin cluster has $O(1)$ neighbors at scale $n$ — that is, the coarsened tessellation looks locally like $\mathbb{Z}^2$ — then the branching process at scale $n$ has $O(1)$ roots, each with offspring $\operatorname{Bin}(3, 1/4)$. The expected progeny is $O(1)$, so the mass gained at scale $n$ is $O(2^n)$. Summing: total mass after $n$ scales is $\sum_{j=0}^n O(2^j) = O(2^n)$.

Combining with the timescale: the time to complete $n$ scales is $\sum_{j=0}^n 2^{j\alpha} \sim 2^{n\alpha}$, so setting $t \sim 2^{n\alpha}$ gives $n \sim \frac{\log t}{\alpha}$ and

$$|C_0(t)| \lesssim 2^n = (2^{n\alpha})^{1/\alpha} \sim t^{1/\alpha}.$$
Remark (the gap — worst-case without $O(1)$ neighbors)

Without the $O(1)$-neighbor property, we must use the worst-case bound: the origin cluster's boundary at scale $n$ is $O(|C_0^{(n-1)}|)$, the mass inherited from all previous scales. Each of these boundary neighbors initiates a branching process whose children each contribute mass $\sim 2^n$. This creates multiplicative feedback: $|C_0^{(n)}| \sim |C_0^{(n-1)}| \cdot 2^n$. Iterating from scale $0$: $$|C_0^{(n)}| \sim \prod_{k=0}^n 2^k = 2^{n(n+1)/2} = 2^{\Theta(n^2)}.$$ Since $t \sim 2^{n\alpha}$ gives $n \sim \frac{\log t}{\alpha}$, this yields $|C_0(t)| \lesssim 2^{\Theta((\log t)^2 / \alpha^2)}$ — subexponential in $t$, but far from the conjectured $t^{1/\alpha}$.

The heuristic justification for $O(1)$ neighbors is translation invariance: at each scale, the tessellation is statistically homogeneous — no cluster is geometrically privileged — so the coarsened lattice should resemble $\mathbb{Z}^2$ with $O(1)$ neighbors per cell. Proving this geometric property is the main near-term goal, and it would simultaneously resolve both the worst-case blowup and the multitype branching obstruction described above.

Conjecture (growth rate)

For the CCA model on $\mathbb{Z}^2$ with full initial occupancy and $\alpha > 0$, $$\mathbb{E}[|C_0(t)|] \asymp t^{1/\alpha}.$$

The case $r(m) = 2^{-\alpha m}$

For the exponential mass-velocity relation $r(m) = 2^{-\alpha m}$, the timescale separation becomes doubly exponential: the time to reach scale $n$ is $\sum_{j=0}^n 2^{\alpha \cdot 2^j} \sim 2^{\alpha \cdot 2^n}$. Setting $t \sim 2^{\alpha \cdot 2^n}$ gives $n \sim \log_2(\log_2 t / \alpha)$, so

$$|C_0(t)| \lesssim 2^n \sim \frac{1}{\alpha}\log_2 t.$$

This ultra-slow logarithmic growth reflects the extreme gridlock in the exponential regime: every additional doubling of cluster mass requires an exponentially longer wait, and the rigorous analysis (presented on the exponential regime page) yields a subexponential upper bound via worst-case neighbor estimates.

Summary of results and status

For the frozen model at $M = 2$, the Galton–Watson domination and almost-sure freezing are rigorous. The multi-scale extension to the unfrozen process is conditional on two inputs: the timescale separation (controlled for large $\alpha$) and the $O(1)$-neighbor property of the tessellation (currently a conjecture, supported by simulation evidence and a translation-invariance heuristic). Proving this geometric property is the main near-term goal.
