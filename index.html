Cluster-Cluster Aggregation on ℤ²

Noam Borgnia · Master's thesis under Prof. Noam Berger, TU Munich, 2026

The Model

We study a stochastic aggregation process on the two-dimensional integer lattice. Begin with particles scattered randomly across the grid — each site is independently occupied with probability p. Every particle starts as its own cluster of mass 1.

Clusters evolve in continuous time. Each cluster of mass m carries an independent exponential clock ringing at rate m−α, where α > 0 is a fixed parameter controlling how mobility depends on size. When a cluster's clock rings, it attempts to translate rigidly by one lattice step in a uniformly random direction.

If the proposed move would cause any site of the cluster to overlap with a site belonging to a different cluster, the two clusters merge: edges are drawn between all overlapping site pairs, the two clusters unite at their current positions (neither moves), and the merged cluster receives a new clock at the combined rate. If a move would overlap with multiple clusters, one is chosen uniformly at random. If there is no overlap, the cluster simply translates.

The parameter α is the key control. At α = 0, all clusters move at the same speed regardless of size — aggregation is fast and democratic. For α > 0, large clusters become nearly immobile while small ones dart around and get absorbed. For α < 0, the situation reverses: large clusters move faster than small ones, producing explosive growth — once a cluster gets big it races around the lattice absorbing everything in its path. This regime is relevant to the question of whether an infinite component can form in finite time.

The Gillespie Algorithm

The simulation is an exact implementation of the continuous-time Markov chain governing the cluster dynamics. Rather than ticking a global clock and checking whether each cluster acts, the Gillespie algorithm exploits the memoryless property of exponential distributions to jump directly from one event to the next.

At any moment, each cluster i of mass mi has an independent exponential clock at rate mi−α. By superposition, the next event across all clusters occurs at rate R = Σ mi−α, and the waiting time to that event is Exp(R). Conditioning on an event occurring, the probability that it was triggered by cluster i is mi−α / R.

Each step of the algorithm therefore proceeds as follows:

  1. Compute the total rate R = Σ mi−α over all clusters.
  2. Advance time by Δt ~ Exp(R).
  3. Select the active cluster A with probability proportional to its rate.
  4. Choose a uniformly random lattice direction and attempt the move-or-merge step.

This is exact: each run produces a valid sample path of the continuous-time process. The efficiency comes from collapsing all individual clocks into a single Poisson process, avoiding the need to track residual waiting times.

Conjectures and Results

Growth rate

Simulations across a range of α values reveal that the mean cluster size at the origin grows as a power law in time:

𝔼[|C₀(t)|] ~ tβ,   where   β ≈ 1/α.

This can be explained by a mean-field heuristic. A cluster of mass m moves at rate m−α and has a boundary of size O(mγ), where γ is the perimeter exponent. Accounting for the density of surrounding clusters via mass conservation, the growth equation yields β = 1/(1 + α − γ).

For low density p, clusters are sparse and tree-like, suggesting γ ≈ 1 (total ramification — every site is a boundary site), which gives β ≈ 1/α. At higher p, clusters are denser from the start and more likely to form compact regions with interior sites, lowering γ and producing slower growth. The dependence of γ on p is an interesting open question.

Infinite components in finite time

A central question in the study of this model is whether an infinite connected component can emerge in finite time. The answer depends critically on α. For α < 0, large clusters move faster than small ones, leading to explosive growth: a large cluster sweeps the lattice at an accelerating rate, and heuristic arguments suggest an infinite component can form in finite time. For large positive α, big clusters are nearly frozen and growth is slow, making finite-time emergence of an infinite component impossible.

The critical value of α separating these two regimes — and the precise nature of the transition — is an active area of research. Prof. Berger's ongoing work addresses the existence and characterization of this phase transition.

Open questions

Several directions remain open:

Cluster geometry. The ramification exponent γ can be measured directly from simulations by computing boundary-to-mass ratios. Understanding how γ depends on p, α, and cluster size would refine the growth-rate prediction and connect to the fractal geometry of DLCA-like processes.

Critical α for infinite components. Determining the sharp threshold in α below which infinite components form in finite time, and characterizing the behavior at criticality, is a fundamental open problem.

Tail behavior. Preliminary simulations suggest that cluster size distributions decay exponentially for all α > 0, with the rate approaching zero as α → 0. The functional form of this decay rate and whether a true power-law regime exists remain to be determined.

Limiting Regime: α → −2

For strongly negative α, a single dominant cluster moves so fast relative to all others that the process effectively reduces to one cluster growing by absorbing stationary particles. In this regime, the cluster geometry depends primarily on the initial density p, and the process resembles classical single-cluster growth models:

At high p, the lattice is nearly full and the dominant cluster grows by filling its neighbors — resembling the Eden model, which produces roughly circular, compact clusters. At moderate p, the cluster explores a random subgraph of occupied sites — potentially resembling percolation cluster exploration, where the geometry is governed by the connectivity threshold. At low p, particles are so sparse that the cluster must wander significantly between absorptions — resembling diffusion-limited aggregation (DLA), which produces highly ramified, fractal structures.
