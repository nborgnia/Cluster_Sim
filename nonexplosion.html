Nonexplosion for $\alpha \geq 0$

Based on an unpublished argument of Prof. Noam Berger (TU Munich, ~2025)

The most basic question about the CCA model is whether clusters remain finite: can an infinite connected component form in finite time? For $\alpha \geq 0$, the answer is no. The argument below, due to Berger, establishes this by bounding the total inter-scale mass flux. The proof is carried out at $\alpha = 0$ (the hardest case); the extension to $\alpha > 0$ is immediate, since larger clusters only move slower.

Setup and notation

We work in dimension $d = 2$ (the argument generalizes). Let $v_k(t)$ denote the density of vertices in clusters of size $k$ at time $t$. By the ergodic theorem applied to the shift-invariant initial measure,

$$v_k(t) = \mathbb{P}(\text{the origin belongs to a cluster of size } k \text{ at time } t).$$

Translation invariance is essential here: it ensures $v_k(t)$ is a deterministic quantity, independent of the realization. Without it, one would need to work on a finite torus and take limits. We have the normalization $\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} v_k(t) = 1$ for all $t$, as long as no infinite component exists.

Smoothed dyadic scales

We group the densities into exponentially growing size scales. A naive partition $W_n = \sum_{k=2^n+1}^{2^{n+1}} v_k$ creates discontinuities when a cluster crosses a scale boundary. To fix this, we smooth: near each transition point $2^{n+1}$, the weight $w_n(k)$ ramps linearly from $1$ to $0$ over a window of width $2^{n+1}/8$, while $w_{n+1}(k)$ ramps up correspondingly. Precisely:

$$w_n(k) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{k - (2^n - 2^n/8)}{2^n/8} & \text{if } 2^n - \tfrac{2^n}{8} \leq k \leq 2^n, \\[6pt] 1 & \text{if } 2^n < k \leq 2^{n+1} - \tfrac{2^{n+1}}{8}, \\[4pt] \dfrac{2^{n+1} - k}{2^{n+1}/8} & \text{if } 2^{n+1} - \tfrac{2^{n+1}}{8} < k \leq 2^{n+1}, \\[6pt] 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

Setting $W_n = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} w_n(k)\, v_k$, the adjacent ramps satisfy $w_n(k) + w_{n+1}(k) = 1$ in every overlap region, giving the partition of unity

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} W_n = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} v_k = 1.$$

Collision rates and the ODE

A cluster at scale $m$ (size $\sim 2^m$) moves at rate $(2^m)^{-\alpha}$. At $\alpha = 0$ this is rate $1$. The number density of scale-$m$ clusters is $W_m / 2^m$ (total mass at that scale divided by mass per cluster). When such a cluster collides with another cluster $C$ at scale $n$, there are three cases:

Case 1: $|C| \ll 2^m$ (small cluster absorbed)

$W_m$ increases by at most $2^m$, and some lower scale $W_j$ ($j < m$) loses the corresponding amount. The merger stays within scale $m$.

Case 2: $|C| \approx 2^m$ (same-scale collision)

This is generically the most common case. $W_m$ decreases by $\sim 2^m$ (the original cluster leaves) and $W_{m+1}$ increases by the same amount (the merged cluster enters the next scale). The net change is $O(2^m)$.

Case 3: $|C| > 2^{m+1}$ (absorbed into a larger cluster)

$W_m$ loses $\sim 2^m$. The cluster at scale $n > m$ gains $\sim 2^m$ in mass, which may push $W_n$ into $W_{n+1}$ at most (by the gradual scale separation — a cluster at scale $n$ absorbing something of size $2^m \ll 2^n$ only changes scale by an amount $\sim 2^m / 2^n \ll 1$).

Define the collision distribution $a_n^{(m)}(t) := \mathbb{P}(\text{collision partner is at scale } n \mid \text{cluster at scale } m \text{ collides})$. Since every collision hits either a finite-scale cluster or (potentially) an infinite component:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n^{(m)} \leq 1 \qquad \text{for all } m, t.$$

The inequality is strict if and only if there is positive probability of colliding with an infinite component — which is precisely what we want to rule out.

The absolute flux bound

Define the inter-scale mass flux $\lambda_{m,n} := $ rate of net mass transfer from scale $m$ to scale $n$. For $n > m$:

$$|\lambda_{m,n}| \leq a_n^{(m)} \cdot W_m + a_m^{(n)} \cdot W_n.$$

Now sum over all pairs:

$$\sum_{n,m} |\lambda_{m,n}| \leq 2 \sum_m \sum_n W_m \cdot a_n^{(m)} \leq 2 \sum_m W_m \cdot \underbrace{\sum_n a_n^{(m)}}_{\leq\, 1} \leq 2 \sum_m W_m = 2.$$
Remark (where $\alpha \geq 0$ enters)

At $\alpha = 0$, all clusters move at rate $1$, and the bound $\sum_n a_n^{(m)} \leq 1$ gives uniform control. For $\alpha > 0$, larger clusters move slower, which only tightens the flux bound. For $\alpha < 0$, the movement rate $(2^m)^{|\alpha|}$ grows with $m$, and the sum becomes $\sum_m W_m \cdot (2^m)^{|\alpha|}$, which can diverge even though $\sum_m W_m = 1$. This is consistent with the existence of infinite components in finite time for $-1 < \alpha < 0$.

Completing the proof

Theorem (Berger, ~2025)

For $\alpha \geq 0$, with shift-invariant initial conditions, there is no infinite connected component in finite time almost surely.

Proof.

Fix a time horizon $T > 0$. Define the integrated flux $\rho_{m,n} := \int_0^T |\lambda_{m,n}(t)|\, dt$. By the flux bound integrated over $[0, T]$:

$$\sum_{n,m} \rho_{m,n} \leq 2T < \infty.$$

Since this double sum is finite, for any $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N_\varepsilon$ such that the total mass transferred to or from scales above $N_\varepsilon$ by time $T$ is at most $\varepsilon$. Therefore:

$$1 - \varepsilon < \sum_{m=0}^{N_\varepsilon} W_m(T) \leq 1.$$

Since this holds for every $\varepsilon > 0$:

$$\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} W_m(T) = 1 \qquad \text{for all } T > 0.$$

All mass remains in finite clusters for all time — no infinite connected component exists.

$\square$

Discussion

The ODE system for the $W_n$ involves an infinite sum of terms that are not sign-definite, so one cannot directly interchange differentiation and summation (neither dominated convergence nor monotone convergence applies). The flux bound sidesteps this entirely: instead of controlling each $\dot{W}_n$ individually, it bounds the total absolute rate of mass movement across all scales. The finiteness of this total flux is enough to conclude that no mass escapes to infinity.

Translation invariance plays a structural role: the density $v_k(t)$ is defined via the ergodic theorem, which requires spatial stationarity. The normalization $\sum_m W_m = 1$ is what makes the flux bound close. Without stationarity, $\sum_m W_m$ could fluctuate spatially, and the argument would need to be run on a finite torus with uniform-in-$L$ estimates.

The proof is sharp in the sense that it fails exactly where it should: at $\alpha < 0$, the flux bound diverges because the movement rate $(2^m)^{|\alpha|}$ grows with scale, and indeed infinite components do form in finite time for $-1 < \alpha < 0$.

For $\alpha > 0$, the flux bound is stronger than needed: the collision rates carry an extra factor of $(2^m)^{-\alpha}$, which suggests one might extract moment bounds $M_p(t) = \sum_k k^p v_k(t) < \infty$ for $\alpha$ large relative to $p$. However, there is a fundamental obstruction: small clusters (singletons in particular) move at rate $O(1)$ regardless of $\alpha$, so they can adjoin to large clusters at a constant rate that no amount of large-cluster slowdown can suppress. The flux bound, which treats all collisions uniformly, cannot distinguish this channel. Obtaining sharper growth bounds therefore requires a different ingredient — specifically, the gridlock mechanism, which guarantees that the supply of small particles near any given cluster is eventually exhausted.
